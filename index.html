Space Rover Challenge


This game demo was created in Unity by the team at Mini Mammoth Games to help promote the 2022 Australian Rover Challenge. Players navigate around the moon, collecting rock samples and constructing additional processing plants to expand your base in an imitation of real-life space rover activities. With the assistance of several digital arts and IT students at Flinders University, the final demo was completed in the span of only a few weeks.

In this project, I assisted in developing the basic interaction system, as well as a collectable tracking framework, using C#. This project marked my first real venture into the Unity Engine, which left me with a lot to learn in not a whole lot of time. One of the biggest challenges I faced involved not only learning the ropes of this engine, but in then understanding it well enough to properly assimilate my own code in accordance with good software principals and the conventions used by the Mini Mammoth team. This environment provided a good test of my own technical skills. It also proved to me the classic software engineering adage: premature optimization is the root of all evil. Indeed, the tight deadline really challenged me to prioritise meeting a deadline over perfecting every line of code. In this way, I was able to foster a more productive mindset that I hope to take forward to all my future projects.

This project provided an excellent opportunity for me to get a feel for working in the games industry, and allowed me to contribute for the first time in a professional capacity to a project that would eventually see full-scale release. It was beyond exciting to be able to contribute to a project that was driven by the creativity of so many skilled artists and designers.

Follow The Path


Follow The Path is a short first-person adventure game set in a medieval fantasy world. The game takes inspiration from similar titles, such as The Stanley Parable, in that the main gimmick revolves around a particularly irritable narrator that the player can listen to or ignore as they see fit. It features two main narrative paths that the player can choose between, as well as several smaller instances of dynamic narration sprinkled throughout both storylines. The project was created using the blueprint system in Unreal Engine 5 by a team consisting of five other IT students in the span of around four months.

In this project, I was responsible for fully designing and implementing the bulk of the content of one of the two main narrative pathways. My pathway had the player collecting a series of ingredients scattered throughout a single enclosed area. These ingredients include a mushroom, a crow's feather, and a ram's bone, all of which have their own associated behaviours that tie into the game’s wacky tone. Once the player has gathered all the ingredients, a door opens, and they're allowed to leave the area by way of an unreasonably difficult platforming section.

This project offered a lot of freedom in the design, in that it could be as weird and wonderful as I wanted, and the more I deviated from the genre, the better it would work for the game. Here, fourth-wall breaks and genre-breaking mechanics were completely on-brand, which made for a completely unique experience where I could fully indulge in any fun and stupid ideas I had. One thing I felt obliged to include was the final platforming section, since a similar section had been created as the tutorial level by another member of the team. It soon became apparent however that the game was not exactly built for platforming, since the jumping and movement controls were so stiff and imprecise, which made for a pretty irritating platforming experience. Instead of just removing the section, I decided to instead have a bit of fun with the project and add a completely out-of-place elevator, complete with some welcoming elevator tunes, ambient lighting, and a very grumpy narrator.

Having full responsibility over an entire chunk of the game also gave me a good feel for the typical workflows involved in game development, and broadened my perspective a bit on the different skill sets needed to sculpt one integrated experience. It also opened my eyes a bit on how best to collaborate on code within a team of programmers.
